Change of Variables in Integrals

Author: Ravi Saripalli  |  Date: 21st May 2026

Consider a simple integral of a function $f(x)$ and how a change of variable of integration is performed.

\[ I = \int \sin(x^2) \, dx \]

Consider a change of variable $y$ such that $y = x^2$:

\begin{align*} \int \sin(y) \qty(\dv{y}{x})^{-1} \, dy = \int \sin(y) \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{y}} \, dy \end{align*}

Notice that we have $x=\pm\sqrt{y}$, but we restrict our focus to the positive branch. Since the absolute slope of the normal to the transformation curve is given by $\left|-(\dv{y}{x})^{-1}\right|$, we can state that a one-dimensional change of variables results in scaling the integrand by the absolute value of this normal slope.

Multivariate Transformations

Consider the double integral over a Cartesian area element:

\[ \iint (x^2 + y^2) \, dx \, dy \]

We introduce a change of variables to polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$, defined by:

\[ x = r \cos(\theta), \quad y = r \sin(\theta) \]

The differential coordinate vectors transform via the forward Jacobian matrix:

\begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} dx \\ dy \end{bmatrix} &= J_{(x,y) \to (r,\theta)} \begin{bmatrix} dr \\ d\theta \end{bmatrix} \\ \text{where } J_{(x,y) \to (r,\theta)} &= \begin{bmatrix} \pdv{x}{r} & \pdv{x}{\theta} \\ \pdv{y}{r} & \pdv{y}{\theta} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) & -r \sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) & r \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \end{align*}

When transitioning from single to multiple variables, a frequent conceptual trap is treating $dx$ and $dy$ as independent scalar quantities that can be multiplied via elementary algebra. Blindly multiplying the expansion formulas for $dx$ and $dy$ yields an incorrect algebraic combination of $dr^{2}$ and $d\theta^2$ terms. In reality, $dxdy$ represents a differential area element on the plane. Under a curved transformation, this rectangular patch deforms into a tilted parallelogram, behaving exactly like a geometric cross product (or wedge product). The cross product naturally eliminates parallel components ($dr \va{r} \cross dr \va{r} = 0$), leaving behind only the joint area element. The Jacobian determinant is the precise geometric tool that measures this local area scaling.

The area of the differential element in the transformed coordinate system can be derived via vector fields:

\begin{align*} d\va{r}_1 &= \pdv{\va{r}}{r}dr = \left(\cos(\theta)\va{i} + \sin(\theta)\va{j}\right)dr \\ d\va{r}_2 &= \pdv{\va{r}}{\theta}d\theta = \left(-r\sin(\theta)\va{i} + r\cos(\theta)\va{j}\right)d\theta \\ dx \, dy \, (\va{i} \cross \va{j}) &= d\va{r}_1 \cross d\va{r}_2 = (r \, dr \, d\theta) \, (\va{i} \cross \va{j}) \\ \text{Taking the norm: } \quad dx \, dy &= r \, dr \, d\theta \end{align*}

The matrix used in this transformation is the Jacobian of the map $(r,\theta) \to (x,y)$. When an inverse mapping is more readily available or easier to differentiate, one can evaluate the determinant of the reverse transformation matrix and exploit the scalar reciprocal identity. This strategy becomes vital when translating between two distinct non-orthogonal grids. Executing this substitution yields the transformed double integral:

\begin{align*} &\iint \qty(r^2 \cos^2(\theta) + r^2 \sin^2(\theta)) \left|\det(J)\right| \, dr \, d\theta \\ &= \iint r^3 \, dr \, d\theta \end{align*}

This framework generalizes to arbitrary dimensions. The absolute determinant of the respective Jacobian matrix tracks the precise spatial scaling of the integrand. If a transformation choice causes the Jacobian determinant to vanish, the transformation becomes invalid at those coordinates; a zero determinant is a definitive structural indicator that the chosen coordinate fields fail to maintain linear independence.

Generalized Coordinate Transformation

The scaling of a differential area element can be generalized to transformations involving non-orthogonal coordinate systems:

\begin{align*} \text{Let } \quad u = f(p,q) \quad &\text{and} \quad v = g(p,q) \\ J_{(u,v) \to (p,q)} &= \begin{bmatrix} \pdv{u}{p} & \pdv{u}{q} \\ \pdv{v}{p} & \pdv{v}{q} \end{bmatrix} \\ \left|\det J_{(p,q) \to (u,v)}\right| &= \frac{1}{\left|\det J_{(u,v) \to (p,q)}\right|} \end{align*}

The differential area elements scale proportionally according to the skew angle of the basis vectors:

\begin{align*} \frac{dp \, dq}{du \, dv} &= \frac{\sin(\alpha)}{\sin(\beta)} \frac{1}{\left|\det J_{(u,v) \to (p,q)}\right|} \\ \text{where } \quad \norm{\va{u} \cross \va{v}} &= \sin(\alpha) \quad \text{and} \quad \norm{\va{p} \cross \va{q}} = \sin(\beta) \end{align*}

Concluding Remarks

The core lesson of this geometric view is that coordinate scaling factors always measure how physical space expands or contracts. When changing variables in standard functions, the absolute determinant $|\det(J)|$ tracks how a region locally stretches, which is why it sits as a multiplier next to our differentials. This behavior generalizes smoothly across curvilinear systems.

In the next section, we shift our focus to evaluating integrals containing the Dirac Delta Function ($\delta$), where coordinate transformations govern distribution behaviors.

Integrands with Dirac Delta function

The Dirac delta function $\delta(x)$ is a generalized function (distribution) characterized by its sifting property under an integral. It is zero for all non-zero arguments, spikes infinitely at the origin, and integrates to unity over the real line. This definition extends naturally to higher dimensions via products of coordinate distributions.

\begin{align*} \delta(x) = 0 \quad \text{for } x \neq 0, \quad \text{and} \quad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(x) \, dx = 1 \end{align*}

Standard distributional evaluations satisfy the following properties:

\begin{align*} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \delta(x-a) f(x) \, dx &= f(a) \\ \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} \delta(x-a, y-b) g(x,y) \, dx \, dy &= g(a,b) \end{align*}

In the simple cases above, the delta arguments are linear offsets of the integration variables. However, when the argument is a complex, continuously differentiable function, an explicit change of variables is required to evaluate the sifting property.

\begin{align*} I &= \int \delta(\phi(x)) f(x) \, dx \\ \text{Let } y &= \phi(x) \implies dy = \abs{\dv{\phi}{x}} \, dx \\ I &= \int \delta(y) \frac{f(x)}{\abs{\dv{\phi}{x}}} \, dy = \left. \frac{f(x)}{\abs{\dv{\phi}{x}}} \right|_{\phi(x)=0} \end{align*}

We now extend this mechanics to two dimensions where the delta function is composed with a multivariable function:

\begin{align*} I = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} \delta(\phi(x, y)) f(x,y) \, dx \, dy \end{align*}

Let $z = \phi(x, y)$. To construct a valid coordinate transformation, we assume the existence of a local single-valued inverse function branch, written as $y = \psi(x,z)$, mapping coordinates from $(x,y) \to (x,z)$.

\begin{align*} J_{(x,z) \to (x,y)} &= \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ \pdv{\psi}{x} & \pdv{\psi}{z} \end{bmatrix} \\ I &= \iint \delta(z) f(x,\psi(x,z)) \abs{\pdv{\psi}{z}} \, dx \, dz \\ &= \int f(x,\psi(x,0)) \abs{\pdv{\psi}{z}(x,0)} \, dx \end{align*}

While the sequence above details a single isolated inverse branch $\psi_i(x,z)$, general non-linear functions $\phi(x,y)=0$ can possess multiple discrete solution roots. To evaluate the total distribution accurately, we must sum over all contributing inverse coordinate branches:

\begin{align*} I &= \iint \delta(z) \sum_i f(x,\psi_i(x,z)) \abs{\pdv{\psi_i}{z}} \, dx \, dz \\ &= \int \sum_i f(x,\psi_i(x,0)) \abs{\pdv{\psi_i}{z}(x,0)} \, dx \end{align*}
