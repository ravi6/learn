Change of Variables in Integrals

Author: Ravi Saripalli  |  Date: May 2026

Consider a simple integral of a function $f(x)$ and how a change of variable of integration is performed in a simple manner.

\begin{align*} I = \int \sin(x^2) dx \\ \end{align*} Consider a change of variable y such that $y = x^2$ \begin{align*} \int \sin(y) \qty{{\dv{y}{x}}}^{-1} dy \\ = \int \sin(y) \frac{1}{2 \sqrt{y}} dy \\ \end{align*} Notice that we have $x=\pm\sqrt{y}$. But we choose positive value. Since we know magnitude of the normal of the curve is $-{(\dv{y}{x})}^{-1}$, we could say change of variable results in scaling the integrand by absolute value of the normal. $$ \text{Consider } \iint (x^2 + y^2) dx dy $$ $$ \text{With change of variables } (r, \theta) $$ $$ x = r cos(\theta) \quad \ y = r sin(\theta)$$ \[ \begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} dx \\ dy \end{bmatrix} &= J_{(x,y) \to (r,\theta)} \begin{bmatrix} dr \\ d\theta \end{bmatrix} \\ \text {where } J_{(x,y) \to (r,\theta)} &= \begin{bmatrix} \pdv{x}{r} \ \ \ \pdv{x}{\theta} \\ \pdv{y}{r} \ \ \ \pdv{y}{\theta} \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) \ \ \ - r \sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) \ \ \ r \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \end{align*} \]

When we move from single to multiple variables, the biggest mistake is treating \(dx\) and \(dy\) as separate numbers that can be multiplied like basic algebra. If you blindly multiply the formulas for \(dx\) and \(dy\) during a change of variables, following the above relations, you get an algebraic mess of \(dr^{2}\) and \(d\theta^2\) terms that makes no sense. The trap is thinking of \(dxdy\) as a simple multiplication. In reality, \(dxdy\) represents the area of a tiny patch on the plane. When coordinates curve, that patch stretches into a tilted parallelogram, behaving exactly like a geometric cross product. The cross product naturally kills off the parallel \(dr^{2}\) and \(d\theta^2\) directions, leaving behind only the actual area element. The Jacobian matrix isn't a magic formula we arbitrarily substitute; it is just the mathematical tool that measures how much that tiny cross-product patch stretched.

The area of differential element in the transformed variables can be determined as follows.

\begin{gather*} dx\ \va{i} = dr \ct\ \va{r} - r \st d\theta\ \va{\theta} \\ dy\ \va{j} = dr \st\ \va{r} + r \ct d\theta\ \va{\theta} \\ dx\ dy\ ( \va{i} \cross \va{j} ) = (r\ dr\ d\theta)\ (\va{r} \cross \va{\theta)} \\ dx\ dy\ \norm{\va{i} \cross \va{j} } = (r\ dr\ d\theta)\ \norm{\va{r} \cross \va{\theta}} \\ \text{since $\va{i}$ is orthogonal to $\va{j}$ and $\va{r}$ is orthogonal to $\va{\theta}$ } \\ dx dy \norm{\va{i}} \norm{\va{j}} sin(\pi/2) = r dr d\theta \norm{\va{r}} \norm{\va{\theta}} sin(\pi/2)\\ dx dy = r dr d\theta \end{gather*}

The square matrix in the above equation is known as the Jacobian of the transformation from $(x,y) \to (r,\theta)$. And it is worth nothing when needed one can use Jacobian of the transform in reverse, and use inverse rule to obtain the determinant in the normal direction. This concept will be eviden when the formula for traslating from one non-orthogonal grid to antother non-orthogonal grid. With this concept firmly bedded we can now write te double integral in the transformed variables as follows.

\begin{align*} J = \begin{bmatrix} \cos(\theta) \ \ \ - r \sin(\theta) \\ \sin(\theta) \ \ \ r \cos(\theta) \end{bmatrix} \\ \end{align*} Taking the geometric scale factor $|\det(J)| = r$: \begin{align*} &\iint \qty{r^2 \cos^2(\theta) + r^2 \sin^2(\theta)} |\det(J)| \, dr \, d\theta \\ &= \iint r^3 \, dr \, d\theta \end{align*}

It follows then that we can generalize multiple integrals on any dimension with change of variables with the construction of Jacobian matrix and determination of its determinant to compute the scaling of integrand in the transformed variable. It is important to understand while the choice of transformation variables and their relation to the original variables is non unique, certain choices that make the determinant zero will result in infeasible transformation for obvious reason. The fact that the determinant of the Jacobian is zero itself is a definitive indicator the transformation variables are not independent.

Generalized coordinate transformation

\begin{align*} \text{let} \quad u &= f(p,q) \quad \text{and } \quad v = g(p,q) \\ J_{(u,v) \to (p,q)} &= \begin{bmatrix} \pdv{u}{p} \ \ \ \pdv{u}{q} \\ \pdv{v}{p} \ \ \ \pdv{v}{q} \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ \det{ J_{(p,q) \to (u,v)} } &= \frac{1} {J_{(u,v) \to (p,q)}} \\ du\ \va{u} &= dp\ \pdv{u}{p} \va{p} + dq \pdv{u}{q} \va{q} \\ dv\ \va{v} &= dp\ \pdv{v}{p} \va{p} + dq \pdv{v}{q} \va{q}\\ du\ dv\ \norm{\va{u} \cross \va{v} } &= (dp\ dq)\ \left( \pdv{u}{p} \pdv{v}{q} - \pdv{u}{q} \pdv{v}{p} \right) \norm{\va{p} \cross \va{q}} \\ &= (dp\ dq) {J_{(u,v) \to (p,q)}} \norm{\va{p} \cross \va{q}} \\ \frac {dp\ dq} {du\ dv} &= \frac {\norm{\va{u} \cross \va{v}}} {\norm{\va{p} \cross \va{q}}} \frac{1}{\det{J_{(u,v) \to (p,q)}}} \end{align*}

Concluding Remarks

The core lesson of this geometric view is that coordinate scaling factors always measure how physical space expands or contracts. When changing variables in standard functions, the absolute determinant $|\det(J)|$ tracks how a region expands, which is why it sits comfortably as a multiplier next to our differentials.

However, this mechanical view faces a massive roadblock when we introduce localized singular point-masses. In the next section, we will shift our focus to evaluating integrals containing the Dirac Delta Function ($\delta$). We will look at why a point-source density behaves inversely to standard spatial area expansion, turning our substitution workflow completely upside down.

Integrands with Dirac Delta function

The definition of Dirac Delta function $\delta(x)$ is that it is zero at all points other than at $x=0$, and its integral over the range of all possible x values is unity. Since the span of the function where it is nonzero is zero, it is inevitable the value of the function at $x=0$ tends to inifinity. Trivial integrals involving Dirac Delta function can be evaluated as follows, noting that Dirac Delta function definition can be extended to higher dimensions.
\begin{align*} \delta(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } x = 0 \\ 0 & \text{if } x \neq 0 \end{cases} \\ \end{align*} With the above definition, some trivial examples of integrals are as follows. \begin{align*} \int \delta(x-a) f(x) dx = f(a) \\ \iint \delta(x-a, y-b) g(x,y) dx dy = g(a,b) \end{align*}
On In the above integrals the argument of Dirac Delta functions are just offset from the variable with respect to the integration. But if indeed the argument is a complex function as shown below, it the change of variable mechanism would come to our rescue.
\begin{gather*} I = \int \delta(\phi(x)) f(x) dx \\ \text{Let } y = \phi(x) \\ \text{Then } I = \int \delta(y) \frac{f(x)}{\abs{\dv{\phi}{x}}} dy \\ = \left. \frac{f(x)}{\abs{\dv{\phi}{x}}} \right|_{y=0} \\ \end{gather*}
\begin{gather*} I = \iint \delta(\phi(x, y)) f(x,y) dx dy \\ \text{Let } z = \phi(x, y) \\ \text{And change of variables from $(x, y)$ to $(x, z)$ } \\ \text{Then } \ J = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ \ \ 0 \\ \pdv{\phi}{x} \ \ \ \pdv{\phi}{y} \end{bmatrix} \\ I = \iint \delta(z) {f(x,y)}{\abs{\pdv{\phi}{y}}} dx dz\\ = \int f(x,y|_{z=0})\abs{\pdv{\phi}{x}}_{z=0} dx \\ I = \int \sum_{i} \left[ \frac{f(x, y)}{\left\vert{} \pdv{\phi}{y} \right\vert{}} \right]_{y=\eta_i(x)} dx \quad \text{where } \phi(x, \eta_i(x)) = 0 \delta(\phi(x, y)) = \sum_{i} \frac{\delta(y - \eta_i(x))}{\left\vert{} \pdv{\phi}{y}(x, \eta_i(x)) \right\vert{}}I = \iint f(x, y) \left( \sum_{i} \frac{\delta(y - \eta_i(x))}{\left\vert{} \pdv{\phi}{y}(x, \eta_i(x)) \right\vert{}} \right) dx dy = \int \sum_{i} \frac{f(x, \eta_i(x))}{\left\vert{} \pdv{\phi}{y}(x, \eta_i(x)) \right\vert{}} dx \end{gather*}
