With no more musings let us derive the distribution of the distance between the eigen values of a symmetric 2x2 square random matrix

Consider \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} x \ \ z \\ z \ \ y \end{bmatrix} \] where x, y and z are random variables and have independant and identical normal distributions with zero mean and standard deviation of $\sigma$ \begin{align*} p(x) = \GA e^{-\half (x/\sigma)^2}\\ p(y) = \GA e^{-\half (y/\sigma)^2}\\ p(z) = \GA e^{-\half (z/\sigma)^2}\\ \end{align*}
Eigen values of the square matrix $A$ can be calculated as follows
\begin{align*} det \left( A - \lambda I \right) &= 0 \\ det \begin{bmatrix} x-\lambda\ &z \\ z\ &y -\lambda \end{bmatrix} &= 0 \\ (x - \lambda) (y - \lambda) - z^2 &= 0 \\ \lambda^2 - \lambda (x + y) + x y - z^2 &= 0 \end{align*} Root of the above equation give the eigen values of interest. \begin{align*} \lambda &= \half {(x + y) \pm \half \sqrt {(x+y)^2 - 4(x y - z^2)}} \end{align*} Now the distance between the two eigen values ($\phi$) is the difference between the two roots and is given by \begin{align*} \phi(x,y,z) = \sqrt {(x-y)^2 + 4z^2} \end{align*}

Evaluation of distribution of $s$

With known distributions of x,y and z we can determine the distribution of $\phi$ which is the function of x, y, and z as per above expression. Sampling of $s$ from the random samples of x, y and z can be achieved with the help of delta function as following.
\begin{align*} p(s) = \intg\intg\intg &p(x)\ p(y)\ p(z) \\ &\delta(\phi(x,y,z) - s)\ dx\ dy\ dz \end{align*}
We can utilize the change of variable technique that was described in Change of Variables in Integration to reduce the above tripple integral to double integral in x and y with the transformation $z$ variable to $\phi$ as follows. Since $phi$ has two roots when $\phi > |x-y|$ we need to account for contribution of both in variable transformation.
\begin{align*} \phi^2 &= (x-y)^2 + 4 z^2 \\ \pdv{\phi}{z} &= 4z / \phi = \frac{2}{\phi} \sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2} \\ p(z) &= \GA e^{-\frac{(\phi^2 - (x-y)^2)}{8\sigma^2}} \\ p(s) &= \intg \intg \int_{|x-y|}^\infty p(x)\ p(y)\ \GA e^{-\frac{(\phi^2 - (x-y)^2)}{8\sigma^2}} \ \delta(\phi - s)\ \\ & \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad 2 \frac{\phi}{2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}} \ dx\ dy\ d\phi \\ \end{align*}
When we change variables from \(z\) to \(\phi \), we are mapping a domain that goes from \(-\infty \) to \(+\infty \) onto a domain that only goes from \(0\) to \(+\infty \) (since a square root is always positive).Because \(z\) is squared (\(\phi^2 = (x-y)^2 + 4z^2\)), every single value of \(\phi \) actually corresponds to two distinct values of \(z\): a positive one (\(+z\)) and a negative one (\(-z\)).If we only use the standard single-variable Jacobian derivative, we are accidentally throwing away half of our integration space (the negative \(z\) values)! Because our original bell curve probability function \(p(z)\) is perfectly symmetrical around zero, the negative half of the graph contains the exact same area as the positive half. Therefore, we simply multiply our single-branch Jacobian by 2 to fully account for both pieces of the domain. The above tripple integral reduces to double integral as the integral with respect to $\phi$ reduces to the integrad evaluated at $\phi=s$.
\begin{align*} p(s) &= \intg \intg p(x)\ p(y)\ \GA e^{-\frac{(s^2 - (x-y)^2)}{8\sigma^2}} 2 \frac{s}{2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{s^2 - (x-y)^2}} \ dx\ dy\ \\ &= \intg \intg p(x)\ p(y)\ \GA e^{-\frac{(s^2 - (x-y)^2)}{8\sigma^2}} 2 \frac{s}{2} \frac{1}{\sqrt{s^2 - (x-y)^2}} \ dx\ dy\ \\ &= \GB \intg \intg e^{-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2\sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{(s^2 - (x-y)^2)}{8\sigma^2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{s^2 - (x-y)^2}} \ dx\ dy\ \\ \end{align*}
Further transformation of variables is necessary to enable analytic evaluation of the integral and the following transformation from x,y to u,v is chosen as follows. \begin{align*} u = x + y \\ v = x - y \end{align*} Noting that transformed variable $v$ is now constrained to (-s,+s) range to confine integrand to real values the integral is now written as follows. It is easy to show the Jacobian of this transformation from (x,y) to (u,v) is 1/2.
\begin{align*} p(s) &= \GB \intg \int_{-s}^{s} e^{-\frac{u^2 + v^2}{4\sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{s^2 - v^2}{8\sigma^2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{s^2 - v^2}} \ \half du\ dv\ \\ &= \GB \half \intg e^{-\frac{u^2} {4\sigma^2}} \ du \int_{-s}^{s} e^{-\frac{s^2+v^2}{8\sigma^2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{s^2 - v^2}} dv\ \\ \end{align*}
Now we have successfully deliniked the double integral integrands, but further transformation of variable $v$ is required to evaluate integral w.r.t to $v$.
\begin{align*} v = s \ sin\theta \\ p(s) &= \GB \half \intg e^{-\frac{u^2} {4\sigma^2}} \ du \int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} e^{- \frac{s^2 (1 + sin^2\theta)}{8\sigma^2}} d\theta\ \\ &= \GB \half \intg e^{-\frac{u^2} {4\sigma^2}} \ du \int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} e^{-\frac{s^2 (3-cos\ 2\theta)}{16\sigma^2}} d\theta\ \\ \end{align*} Noting that integrand in $\theta$ integral is symmetric about $\theta=0$ and rescaling theta with factor of 2, it is easy to rewrite the integral as follows
\begin{align*} p(s) &= \GB \half \intg e^{-\frac{u^2} {4\sigma^2}} \ du \int_{0}^{\pi} e^{-\frac{s^2 (3-cos\ \theta)}{16\sigma^2}} d\theta\ \\ &= \GB \half e^{-\frac{3s^2}{16\sigma^2}} \intg e^{-\frac{u^2} {4\sigma^2}} \ du \int_{0}^{\pi} e^{\frac{s^2}{16\sigma^2} \cos\ \theta} d\theta\ \\ \end{align*}
The second integral is in the form of the Modified Bessel Function of first Kind representing infinite series as shown below. \[ I_0(x) = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\pi e^{(x\ cos\ \theta)} d\theta \]
\begin{align*} p(s) &= \GB \half e^{-\frac{3 s^2}{16\sigma^2}} \ \pi \ I_0\left(\frac{s^2}{16\sigma^2}\right) \intg e^{-\frac{u^2} {4\sigma^2}} \ du \\ \end{align*}
Finally the integral with respect $u$ is in the form of a Normal distribution function whose integral is well known. And finally the distribution $s$ is as follows.
\begin{align*} p(s) &= \GB \half e^{-\frac{3s^2}{16\sigma^2}} \ \pi \ I_0\left(\frac{s^2}{16\sigma^2}\right) \sqrt{4\pi\sigma^2} \\ &= \GB \half e^{-\frac{3s^2}{16\sigma^2}} \ \pi \ I_0\left(\frac{s^2}{16\sigma^2}\right) \sqrt{4\pi\sigma^2} \\ &= \frac{s}{2\sqrt{2}\sigma^2} e^{-\frac{3s^2}{16\sigma^2}} \ I_0\left(\frac{s^2}{16\sigma^2}\right) \\ \end{align*}

Intuitive Note on the Factor of 2:
When we change variables from $z$ to $\phi$, we are mapping a domain that goes from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$ onto a domain that only goes from $|x-y|$ to $+\infty$ (since a square root is always positive). Because $z$ is squared ($\phi^2 = (x-y)^2 + 4z^2$), every single value of $\phi$ actually corresponds to two distinct values of $z$: a positive one ($+z$) and a negative one ($-z$).

If we only use the standard single-variable Jacobian derivative, we are accidentally throwing away half of our integration space (the negative $z$ values)! Because our original bell curve probability function $p(z)$ is perfectly symmetrical around zero, the negative half of the graph contains the exact same area as the positive half. Therefore, we simply multiply our single-branch Jacobian by 2 to fully account for both pieces of the domain.

The above triple integral reduces to a double integral as the integral with respect to $\phi$ reduces to the integrand evaluated at $\phi=s$.

Evaluation of the Distribution of $s$

With the known probability distributions of $x$, $y$, and $z$, we can determine the distribution of their eigenvalue spacing $\phi(x,y,z)$. Sampling the exact spacing distance $s$ from the random matrix elements is achieved using a Dirac delta function as a constraint inside the joint probability integral: \[ p(s) = \intg\intg\intg p(x)\ p(y)\ p(z)\ \delta(\phi(x,y,z) - s)\ dx\ dy\ dz \] To evaluate this, our motivation is to transform the integration variable from $z$ to our spacing variable $\phi$ using the direct forward relationship: \[ \phi = \sqrt{(x-y)^2 + 4z^2} \] Differentiating $\phi$ directly with respect to $z$ gives a clean forward derivative: \[ \pdv{\phi}{z} = \frac{4z}{\phi} \implies dz = \frac{\phi}{4z}\, d\phi \]

The Fork in the Road: Managing the Non-Unique Mapping

As soon as we attempt to substitute our old variable $z$ away, we hit a mathematical hurdle. Rearranging the spacing formula to express $z$ in terms of our new coordinate $\phi$ requires a square root: \[ 4z^2 = \phi^2 - (x-y)^2 \implies z = \pm \half \sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2} \] Because of the $\pm$ sign, our chosen coordinate change is not a simple one-to-one transformation. For every single value of our new distance metric $\phi$, there are two distinct physical pathways in our original $z$ space: a Positive Branch ($z \ge 0$) and a Negative Branch ($z \le 0$). To handle this non-unique transformation rigorously without dropping any integration space, we must process both pathways in parallel.

Path 1: The Positive Branch ($z \ge 0$)

On this pathway, $z = +\half \sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}$. Let us track how the boundaries behave:
  • When $z = 0$, the minimum possible value for $\phi$ is $\sqrt{(x-y)^2} = |x-y|$.
  • When $z \to \infty$, then $\phi \to \infty$.
Substituting this positive root into our differential gives the standard absolute Jacobian element: \[ dz = \frac{\phi}{4|z|}\, d\phi = \frac{\phi}{2\sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}}\, d\phi \] This branch integrates smoothly over forward-running limits from $|x-y|$ to $\infty$: \[ \text{Branch}_1 = \int_{|x-y|}^{\infty} p(x)\, p(y)\, p(+z(\phi))\, \delta(\phi - s)\, \frac{\phi}{2\sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}}\, d\phi \]

Path 2: The Negative Branch ($z \le 0$)

On this parallel pathway, $z = -\half \sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}$. Tracking the boundaries as $z$ moves forward from $-\infty$ up to $0$:
  • When $z = -\infty$, our positive distance metric starts at $\phi = \infty$.
  • When $z = 0$, the boundary finishes at $\phi = |x-y|$.
Because our slope is falling on this side, the spatial differential explicitly tracks the negative orientation of the line: $dz = -\frac{\phi}{4|z|}\, d\phi$. This leaves us with an integral that initially runs backward from $\infty$ down to $|x-y|$: \[ \text{Branch}_2 = \int_{\infty}^{|x-y|} p(x)\, p(y)\, p(-z(\phi))\, \delta(\phi - s)\, \left(-\frac{\phi}{2\sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}}\right) d\phi \] To straighten these integration boundaries out so they run from smallest to largest, we must flip the limits. Flipping an integral's limits introduces a second negative sign ($\int_a^b = -\int_b^a$). This double-negative cancels out perfectly: \[ \text{Branch}_2 = + \int_{|x-y|}^{\infty} p(x)\, p(y)\, p(-z(\phi))\, \delta(\phi - s)\, \frac{\phi}{2\sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}}\, d\phi \]

Collapsing the Sum into a Multiplier

The total probability distribution is the combined sum of both pathways: $p(s) = \text{Branch}_1 + \text{Branch}_2$.

Now we observe a beautiful geometric handshake. First, the limit-flipping math has forced both branches to span the exact same forward integration limits ($|x-y|$ to $\infty$) with the exact same absolute Jacobian factor. Second, because our original Gaussian probability density function $p(z)$ relies strictly on $z^2$, the negative sign on the second branch root vanishes ($(-z)^2 = (+z)^2$).

Because the two integration pathways carry identical physical weight over the exact same domain, the formal summation collapses into a simple global multiplier of 2: \begin{align*} p(s) &= \intg \intg \int_{|x-y|}^{\infty} p(x)\ p(y)\ \GA e^{-\frac{(\phi^2 - (x-y)^2)}{8\sigma^2}}\ \delta(\phi - s)\ \left[ 2 \cdot \frac{\phi}{2\sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}} \right] dx\ dy\ d\phi \\ &= \intg \intg \int_{|x-y|}^{\infty} p(x)\ p(y)\ \GA e^{-\frac{(\phi^2 - (x-y)^2)}{8\sigma^2}}\ \delta(\phi - s)\ \frac{\phi}{\sqrt{\phi^2 - (x-y)^2}}\ dx\ dy\ d\phi \end{align*} The 2 from the dual-branch summation has cleanly cancelled out the $\half$ inside the single-branch Jacobian.

Reduction to the Double Integral

Finally, the Dirac delta function fires, instantly collapsing the $\phi$ integral by evaluating the entire integrand at the exact pinpoint constraint of $\phi = s$. Because the sampling point $s$ must fall inside the valid integration boundaries, this evaluation enforces the physical condition that $s \ge |x-y|$ (or conversely, $(x-y)^2 \le s^2$).

Substituting $\phi = s$ and grouping our standard normalization constants into our macro definition yields our definitive double integral: \begin{align*} p(s) &= \GB \intg \intg e^{-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2\sigma^2}} e^{-\frac{s^2 - (x-y)^2}{8\sigma^2}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{s^2 - (x-y)^2}}\ dx\ dy \end{align*} where the integration domain is now implicitly bound to regions where $(x-y)^2 \le s^2$.

Modified Bessel Function of First Kind

\begin{align*} I_0 (z) = \int_{0}^{\pi} e^{z \ct}\ d\theta \end{align*} Expanding the integrand as infinite series with Taylor Series expansion around $z=0$ \begin{align*} I_0 (z) = \int_{0}^{\pi} \sum_{i=0}^{\infty} \frac{(z\ct)^i}{i!} \ d\theta \end{align*} To further simplify we need to derive the following general integral \begin{align*} C_n &= \int_0^\pi \ctn{n} \ d\theta \\ &=\left[ \st \ctn{n-1} \right]_0^\pi + (n - 1) \int_0^\pi \stn{2} \ctn{n-2} \ d\theta \\ &= 0 + (n - 1) \int_0^\pi \left( 1 - \ctn{2} \right) \ctn{n-2} \ d\theta \\ &= (n-1) C_{n-2} - (n-1) C_n \\ \end{align*} And this gives us the recursive relation of the integrals that can be exploited \[ C_n = \frac{n-1}{n} C_{n-2} \] Ginve that $C_1=\int_0^\pi \ct d\theta=0$, it is easy to see all $C_n$ terms where $n$ is odd would also be zero due the recursion. Then we can deduce that the $C_n$ value for any even value of $n$ can be written as follows. \begin{align*} C_n = \frac {\prod_{i=1}^{n} 2i-1} {\prod_{i=1}^{n} 2(i-1)} \end{align*}
